Course Goals


1. Consider the role of rhetoric, technology, and ethics in acts of making.

Though rhetoric is primarily viewed as a linguistic tool, it is really a part of all communication. Visual communication involves rhetoric just as much as written/spoken communication. Graphic design presents an interesting blend of written and visual rhetoric; a picture may speak a thousand words, but you can still add some more if you like. You can see my attempts to use both forms of rhetoric in my logos and my "Good Design" project, where I've used symbols and imagery to communicate something about myself and reinforce my argument respectively. In terms of ethics, I consider it very important to keep my work mostly original; ergo, I have completely avoided generating any assets with AI.

2. Leverage Design Principles.

The four basic design principles - contrast, repetition, alignment, and proximity - have been at the front of my mind with all of my work. Since I'm personally partial to the color blue, I've frequently contrasted that with orange, red, and white to make sure it looks good in context. I've also established consistent formatting within my projects, and I've taken special care to match up the sizes and alignments of similar assets. In consideration of proximity, I've made sure nothing is too far away from what it relates to on the page, and I've been careful not to leave too much empty space as well.

3. Engage with others in a workshop environment.

I've always been prepared to provide good feedback and accept it from others during class. With their help, I've definitely gotten a better idea of the quality and direction of my work. As a critically-minded person, I feel I've also done a good job providing my peers with solid advice, especially when it comes to prose.



4. Practice revision within and across media forms.

Although I've lacked the time to revise most of my works, the UX research study resulted in some excellent revision from my three logos. The feedback I got from my participants was very helpful, and I'm much more proud of the end result of that project than I was with any of the prior logos. I'm always looking to better myself, so I'm ready and willing to revise my other work if I'm unsatisfied with it.

5. Learn to present your work.

Although my UX research study presentation reminded me that I do better at presenting with somewhat of a script to keep me on-track, I feel I've confidently shared my work in smaller contexts. I'm good at sharing my thought process with my peers and accepting what they have to say regarding my efforts. Even when I don't agree with my audience, it's still helpful to know what they think.
